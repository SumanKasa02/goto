Information Security and Cyber law

Short Answer Questions

1. What challenges are we facing to create a digital society in the Nepalese context?

The major challenges in creating a digital society in Nepal include limited internet accessibility in rural areas, low levels of digital literacy, insufficient infrastructure, high costs of internet services, lack of adequate cybersecurity measures, and regulatory issues. Additionally, the digital divide between urban and rural populations, economic constraints, and political instability also pose significant barriers to the development of a digital society in Nepal.

2. What is computer ethics? What are the commandments of computer ethics?

Computer ethics refers to the set of moral principles that guide the behavior of individuals when using computers and technology. The commandments of computer ethics include:

  • Do not use other’s password
  • Do not steal other’s information
  • Do not use computers to harm others
  • Do not disclose other’s secrecy
  • Do not spread viruses in the network
  • Do not copy licensed software without permission
  • Do not alter others' data
  • Do not take possession of other people's intellectual property
  • Do not misuse electronic transactions
  • Do not use computers to bear false witness

3. Discuss the role of computer ethics in the formation of a secure and reliable digital society.

Computer ethics plays a crucial role in ensuring that individuals use computers responsibly, thereby creating a safe and trustworthy digital environment. By adhering to ethical principles, users can help prevent cybercrimes, protect personal data, and ensure respect for others' privacy and intellectual property. This fosters a sense of accountability and trust, making the digital space more secure and reliable for everyone.

4. Explain the basic principles/components of information security.

The basic principles of information security are:

  • Confidentiality: Ensuring that information is accessible only to authorized individuals or systems.
  • Integrity: Ensuring that data is accurate, complete, and protected from unauthorized modifications.
  • Availability: Ensuring that information is accessible when required, without any hindrance or delay.

5. Define cyber law. What are the major fields of cyber law?

Cyber law is the area of law that deals with legal issues related to the use of computers, internet, and other digital technologies. It covers various aspects of online activities, including cybercrimes, digital contracts, intellectual property, and online privacy. The major fields of cyber law include:

  • Cybercrimes
  • Digital contracts and transactions
  • Intellectual property law
  • Data protection and privacy law
  • Cybersecurity law

6. How do you know that your computer has been the victim of a malware attack?

Signs that your computer may have been infected with malware include:

  • Slower performance or frequent crashes
  • Unexpected pop-up ads or windows
  • Unusual activity or files on your system
  • Increased network activity
  • Disabled security software or firewall
  • Unusual or unknown programs running in the background

7. How can you protect yourself and your computer system from cybercrime?

To protect yourself from cybercrime, you should:

  • Use strong passwords and update them regularly
  • Install reliable antivirus and anti-malware software
  • Keep your operating system and applications updated
  • Be cautious about sharing personal information online
  • Use encrypted communication channels (e.g., VPNs)
  • Regularly back up important data
  • Be aware of phishing and scam tactics

8. What are the advantages and disadvantages of digital signatures?

The advantages of digital signatures include:

  • Ensuring authenticity and non-repudiation of digital documents
  • Enhancing security by preventing unauthorized alterations
  • Supporting faster and more efficient electronic transactions

The disadvantages include:

  • Vulnerability to attacks if the private key is compromised
  • Dependence on technology and infrastructure
  • Complexity and cost involved in obtaining and managing digital certificates

9. Explain various areas that are generally covered in cyber law.

Cyber law typically covers areas such as:

  • Cybercrimes (e.g., hacking, phishing, identity theft)
  • Intellectual property rights (e.g., copyright, patents, trademarks)
  • Online contracts and agreements
  • Data protection and privacy laws
  • Digital signatures and authentication
  • Cybersecurity laws

10. Explain cyber law in Nepal with major provisions included.

Cyber law in Nepal is governed by "The Electronic Transaction Ordinance, 2005." Some of the major provisions included are:

  • Regulations for electronic transactions and contracts
  • Provisions for digital signatures and electronic authentication
  • Intellectual property protection for online content
  • Data protection and privacy regulations
  • Provisions for the prevention of cybercrimes

11. List out the major objectives of ICT Policy 2072.

The major objectives of the ICT Policy 2072 are:

  • To declare IT as a high-priority sector and make it practical
  • To establish knowledge-based industries and increase employment
  • To make public information and services inclusive and effective through e-government
  • To increase the productive use of IT in social, economic, and business organizations
  • To enhance access to international IT developments
  • To develop the Government Integrated Data Center (GIDC) as IT infrastructure

Long Answer Questions

Long Answer Questions

1. Explain at least ten activities that are considered as cybercrime.

Cybercrime refers to illegal activities conducted using computers or the internet. These activities are often targeted at individuals, businesses, or governments and can cause significant harm. Below are ten common activities considered as cybercrime:

  • Hacking: Unauthorized access to a computer or network to steal, modify, or delete data. This can be done for financial gain, data theft, or malicious purposes.
  • Identity Theft: Fraudulently obtaining and using someone else's personal information (e.g., social security number, bank account details) for illegal activities such as opening accounts or making unauthorized transactions.
  • Phishing: Sending deceptive emails or messages to trick users into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card details, and social security numbers. Often used for identity theft or financial fraud.
  • Online Fraud and Scams: Deceptive schemes conducted over the internet to defraud individuals or organizations. Examples include fake job offers, lottery scams, and online shopping frauds.
  • Malware Distribution: The act of spreading malicious software (malware) such as viruses, worms, or ransomware to disrupt, damage, or steal information from a system or network.
  • Cyberbullying and Harassment: The use of online platforms to harass, threaten, or intimidate individuals. This can involve sending offensive messages, spreading rumors, or posting hurtful content about someone.
  • Denial of Service (DoS) Attacks: An attack in which a network or website is flooded with an excessive amount of traffic, causing it to crash and become unavailable to legitimate users.
  • Spreading Viruses: The creation and distribution of viruses that can infect and damage computers, steal information, or make a system unusable.
  • Data Breaches: Unauthorized access to or theft of sensitive data such as personal information, credit card details, or confidential business information from a system or database.
  • Online Child Exploitation: The creation, distribution, or possession of child pornography, exploitation, or abuse of minors through the internet. This is one of the most serious forms of cybercrime.

2. What do you mean by malicious software? Explain any ten malicious software attacks.

Malicious software, commonly referred to as malware, is software intentionally designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems. Malware can perform a variety of harmful actions, including stealing data, damaging files, or rendering systems unusable. Below are ten types of malicious software attacks:

  • Viruses: Malicious programs that attach themselves to clean files and spread to other files or systems. They can corrupt data, cause system crashes, or even make the system unusable.
  • Worms: Self-replicating malware that spreads across networks without needing a host file. Worms often exploit security vulnerabilities in systems to spread.
  • Trojans: Malware that disguises itself as a legitimate program or file to trick users into executing it. Once activated, it can allow cybercriminals to gain control over the system.
  • Spyware: Software that secretly collects information about a user's activities, such as browsing history, keystrokes, and passwords, without the user's knowledge.
  • Ransomware: A type of malware that locks the victim's system or encrypts their files and demands a ransom for restoring access to the system or data.
  • Adware: Unwanted software that automatically displays or downloads advertisements to a computer. While not always malicious, adware can degrade system performance and compromise privacy.
  • Rootkits: Malicious software designed to hide the existence of certain processes or programs from normal detection methods. Rootkits enable attackers to gain unauthorized access to the system.
  • Keyloggers: Software that records keystrokes made by the user, often used to capture sensitive information such as passwords and credit card numbers.
  • Botnets: Networks of infected computers controlled by cybercriminals to carry out large-scale attacks such as Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks, spamming, or stealing data.
  • Phishing Emails: Fraudulent emails that appear to come from legitimate sources, designed to trick the recipient into clicking malicious links or downloading harmful attachments that install malware on their system.

3. Explain intellectual property along with its types in detail. What legal provisions do we have in Nepal to preserve intellectual property rights?

Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind, including inventions, literary works, artistic works, designs, symbols, and names used in commerce. These creations are protected by law to give the creator exclusive rights over their use for a specified period. The main types of intellectual property are:

  • Copyright: Protection granted to the creators of original works of authorship such as books, music, films, and software. Copyright ensures that the creator has the exclusive right to use, distribute, or license the work.
  • Trademarks: Protects words, phrases, symbols, logos, or designs that distinguish goods or services of one party from others in the market. Trademarks help in brand recognition and prevent confusion among consumers.
  • Patents: A form of protection granted for new inventions or processes that offer a novel solution to a particular problem. A patent gives the inventor exclusive rights to the invention for a specified period, typically 20 years.
  • Trade Secrets: Confidential business information that provides a competitive edge, such as formulas, processes, or proprietary knowledge. Trade secrets are protected by non-disclosure agreements and confidentiality clauses.

In Nepal, intellectual property rights are protected by various laws, including:

  • The Copyright Act, 2059 (2002): Protects the rights of creators of literary, artistic, and musical works.
  • The Patent, Design and Trademark Act, 1965: Provides protection for patents, industrial designs, and trademarks.
  • The Intellectual Property Policy of Nepal, 2075 (2019): Aims to promote the protection of intellectual property rights and facilitate the commercialization of innovations and creativity in Nepal.

4. Explain digital signature along with its working mechanism.

A digital signature is a cryptographic technique used to verify the authenticity and integrity of digital messages or documents. It provides a secure way to sign electronic documents and ensures that the message has not been tampered with during transmission. Digital signatures are based on asymmetric encryption, which involves two keys:

  • Private Key: A secret key used to encrypt the digital signature. This key is kept private by the signer.
  • Public Key: A public key that anyone can use to verify the authenticity of the digital signature.

The working mechanism of a digital signature is as follows:

  1. The sender generates a hash value (a unique digital fingerprint) of the message or document.
  2. The sender encrypts the hash value with their private key, creating the digital signature.
  3. The signed message and the digital signature are sent to the recipient.
  4. The recipient decrypts the digital signature using the sender's public key to retrieve the hash value.
  5. The recipient then generates their own hash of the received message and compares it with the decrypted hash value. If they match, the message is authentic, and its integrity is intact.

5. Explain any ten policies included in ICT Policy 2072 Nepal.

The ICT Policy 2072 of Nepal aims to promote the use of information technology (IT) for economic growth, social development, and government efficiency. Some key policies included in the ICT Policy are:

  • National Broadband Network: To develop and expand a national broadband network to increase internet access across Nepal.
  • Promote E-Government: To implement e-government systems to make public administration more efficient and transparent.
  • Universal Access to ICT: To ensure that all citizens, including those in remote areas, have access to ICT services.
  • Digital Literacy: To improve digital literacy among citizens, especially in rural areas, by promoting IT education and training.
  • Cybersecurity: To enhance cybersecurity measures and protect critical infrastructures, businesses, and personal data from cyber threats.
  • Promote ICT-based Entrepreneurship: To encourage the establishment of ICT-based startups and businesses to boost the economy.
  • Support for ICT in Education: To integrate ICT into the education system to enhance learning and teaching methods.
  • Data Protection and Privacy: To protect individuals' personal data and ensure privacy in digital transactions and communication.
  • Public-Private Partnerships: To foster collaboration between the government and private sectors for ICT development.
  • Cloud Computing and Digital Payment Systems: To promote the use of cloud computing and digital payment platforms to modernize business processes and financial transactions.
